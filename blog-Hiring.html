Who Should you Hire: Employee, Freelancer, or Agency?

  • 04 Feb 2025
  • Corporate

In today’s fast-paced and competitive business world, companies often face the dilemma of how to meet their HR or project needs. Do you hire a full-time employee, bring on a freelancer, or outsource to an agency? Each option comes with its own set of benefits and challenges, and the right choice depends largely on your company's specific needs. Here’s a breakdown of when to consider each option and the potential pros and cons.

As of recent estimates, the average age of Ethiopia is around 19.5 years, making the nation feel like a fiery teenager who doesn’t always listen to its parents. With over 60% of the population under 25 years old, Ethiopia’s workforce is young, eager, and full of potential—but also still learning and growing. With the literacy rate hovering between 52% and 55%, Ethiopia finds itself in 6th grade in the global classroom—learning rapidly but still with much to master. In contrast, countries like Finland, Japan, and Germany, boasting nearly 100% literacy, would be considered to be in "university" or "graduate school." This educational backdrop means that businesses must navigate a workforce that, while brimming with potential, may require additional investment in training and development. Due to this and many other factors like finance, efficiency, business secrecy, control… finding and retaining the right fit for a position is no easy task, especially in a startup and scale-up environment, who often lack the resources to effectively manage the hiring and training process on their own.

Hiring an Employee: When Long-Term Investment is Key

Hiring an employee can be a great option when you’re looking for someone who can contribute to the long-term success of your company. Employees are fully integrated into your business culture, align with your team’s vision, and offer the stability of knowing they’ll be around for the long haul. If your company is growing and you need someone to manage ongoing tasks or long-term projects, an employee is often the best choice.

However, this option comes with its own investment of time and resources—recruitment, onboarding, and ongoing training. It’s a bit like planting a tree—it takes time to grow and establish. Employees also come with fixed costs such as salaries, benefits, and overhead, so for short-term or fluctuating needs, they might not be the most cost-effective solution. Especially in a country like Ethiopia, where the workforce is young and still developing skills, businesses might find themselves facing additional training and mentoring costs to bring employees up to speed.

It’s a no-brainer that every business, from small startups to larger “Optimus Prime” level organizations, faces difficulty finding the right person for their needs. The hiring process is often a tricky challenge that can consume more time and energy than actually focusing on the company’s competitive edge in the market. For startups and scale-ups, finding the right fit is even harder, making it difficult to maintain a clear vision while juggling HR issues.

For instance, Hiring a creative person for a non-creative business can be like caging a dog in a bird box (it just barks when you really need it to sing). Also creative personnel thrive when they have a strong creative director to guide and refine their ideas, and if your business is not creative at its core, it can be a challenge to provide that structure. Without the right leadership, managing creative talent can feel like trying to put together a puzzle without all the pieces—without a clear direction, it’s easy for creativity to get lost in the process. This lack of experienced leadership can sometimes lead to frustration for both the employee and the company.

Onboarding Freelancers: The Flexible and Cost-Effective Solution for Short-Term Needs

Freelancers: የመስቀል ወፍ of the workforce. They swoop in, sprinkle their magic on your project, and then disappear into the sunset when the job is done. They’re great if you need a specialized skill for a short-term project or a burst of extra help. Think of them like the ride drivers of the business world—they get you to your destination quickly, but you’re not expecting a long-term relationship.

But, just like that one ride driver who won’t give you the receipt, freelancers can be a little unpredictable. One minute they’re excited about the project, and the next, they’re off to their next gig, leaving you with nothing but the sound of “የደወሉላቸው ደንበኛ …”. The working hours may not align with your team’s, and you might have to deal with the classic freelancer behavior—dodgy receipts, missed deadlines, and, let’s face it, a tendency to “ghost” you when the work gets tough.

And here’s the tricky part: if your business has long-term goals, getting a freelancer to align with them is like convincing a traffic police you passed on a yellow light. Unlike employees, who become part of your company culture and grow with your mission, freelancers work on a per-project basis. If you want them to stay on the same page as your company’s vision, you’ll need to invest a lot more in frequent check-ins, strategic briefings, and sometimes, a bit of financial motivation (higher rates). Over time, what started as a “cheaper” option can turn into an expensive and exhausting juggling act.

Partnering with an Agency: The Strategic and Scalable Choice

Agencies can often be the best option when you need a comprehensive and integrated solution. Agencies offer a team of specialists, bringing together diverse skills under one roof. If your project requires a broad range of expertise—such as web development, branding, marketing, and design all together—an agency can handle it all.

Agencies provide structure, streamlined processes, and an established team, which means you don’t have to worry about recruiting multiple freelancers or hiring employees for various roles. While the upfront cost might be higher than a freelancer or employee, you often get a polished, cohesive result delivered faster and with fewer headaches. You also benefit from their project management skills, ensuring things stay on track and within budget.

While working with an agency might feel a reliable way, they can sometimes feel less integrated with your company’s culture and day-to-day operations. Since they work with multiple clients, you might not get the same level of deep, personal commitment that an in-house employee would bring. It’s kind of like dating someone who’s always “busy”—they’ll get the job done, but you might not always be their number one priority.

But in Ethiopia, where the workforce is young and rapidly growing, agencies can be an ideal option to bridge the gap between talent and experience, providing scalable solutions and a highly skilled team without the burden of managing each individual separately.

When to Choose Which Option?

Hire an Employee:

When your needs are long-term, core to your business, and require someone to grow with the company. This is ideal for full-time roles where consistency and cultural fit are key.

Bring on a Freelancer:

When you need specialized expertise for a short-term project or an additional pair of hands to get things done quickly. Freelancers are great when your needs are project-based and don’t require ongoing work. 

Outsource to an Agency:

When you need a comprehensive solution, fast scalability, and a range of expertise—all delivered efficiently. Agencies are especially useful when a project requires multiple skill sets or a larger team to execute.
